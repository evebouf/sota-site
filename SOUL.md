# SOUL

A publication dedicated to the ongoing evolution of San Francisco — an attempt to expand on conversations surrounding why and how cities are built, and what social, functional, or symbolic needs they satisfy.

## What if we did away with that distinction?

We're often presented with competing ideas of old versus new, as if there is not value in both. We see it in all facets of our everyday lives — in the way people talk about neighborhoods, about buildings, about who belongs where. But what if we did away with that distinction? What if yesterday's ideologies and tomorrow's innovations could coexist — not in tension, but in conversation?

This is what State of the Art attempts. Not a resolution, but a space. A space for writers, architects, and citizens alike, to imagine a city that holds all of its contradictions at once.

## Why San Francisco

San Francisco is a cultural phenomenon, deeply embedded in technology and society. It provides a platform for innovation, conversation, and community building. But it is also a place where you can walk four blocks and feel like you've crossed into a different city entirely — from the quiet of the Richmond to the buzz of the Mission, from the salt air at Fort Point to the particular hush of a used bookstore on Montgomery Street.

We believe cities, like all social institutions, arise from social needs. The question is which needs, and whose, and how we choose to meet them. That is the work.

## On tactility

In a world that's increasingly online in the way that we operate in all areas of life — work, relationships, interests — tactility feels more significant now, than ever. This edition aims to explore process: it asks, how can the method of creation inform a city's meaning? What can traditional, craft-informed methods of building teach us? And how can we reinterpret these traditional methods and ways of working to bring them into a new era?

This publication lives on a screen but it wants to feel like paper. The grain, the fold marks, the texture of ink — these are not decorative choices. They are an insistence that how something is made matters as much as what it says.

## What we show

All of it. The beauty, the grit, and the ugly.

San Francisco is a city of pockets. Each neighborhood carries its own atmosphere, its own rhythm, its own version of the story. We want to move through as many of these pockets as we can — not to flatten them into a single narrative, but to hold them side by side and see what emerges.

The golden light on the Transamerica Pyramid at sunset. The switchbacks of Lombard at blue hour, every window lit. The Golden Gate from Fort Mason, small and impossibly red against the gradient of the sky. We are interested in the city as it actually is — layered, contradictory, always becoming.

## Values

**One must be absolutely modern.** Not modern as in new for the sake of new, but modern as in present — alive to this moment, willing to look clearly at what is happening and ask what it means.

**Counter-entropic will.** Everything decays. Progress is not an arrow — it is a choice, made again and again, to build and maintain and care for the things we've built. The interesting question is not what the future will look like, but what we are willing to sustain.

**Infrastructure for a generation.** City Lights Bookstore was never just a bookstore. It was a place where a generation of writers found each other, challenged each other, made something that outlasted all of them. The best things built in cities function this way — they create conditions for things that don't exist yet.

**The city is not an island.** San Francisco's problems and possibilities exist at the scale of the Northern California Megaregion — Sacramento to Santa Cruz, Napa to San Jose. We think about the city at the scale it actually operates.

## Manifesto

STATE OF THE ART, or SOTA ZINE is an indefensible project born out of the convictions of a megalomaniac. These convictions run as follows: 1) No one reads anymore. 2) No one values good design. 3) No one cares. 4) We will die if we don't do the work.

SOTA ZINE believes in velocity and heat; SOTA ZINE believes in singular genius; SOTA ZINE believes that some things are better than others; SOTA ZINE believes in doing the work; SOTA ZINE believes in making new things; SOTA ZINE believes in tomorrow.

**Red Meat.** We should do things that look GOOD. Even an inside joke which operates on multiple levels should be legible to the drooling median social media user as, simply, "cool." This doesn't mean we dumb things down; it just means that everything should also work at some obvious level.

**All Killer No Filler.** We should strive to make everything we touch excellent — according to our very high standards. The goal should always be excellence in prose, visuals, ideas, execution. We obsess over details. We do not accept anything — a phrase, a design choice, a title — that doesn't make sense. In this we will never be satisfied, of course. That is our curse.

**Faber Ludens.** Maker at play. We are doing our life's work; how could we not have fun.

**Sharp Lines, No Approximation.** Everything should be sharp. It should be in focus. We never allow ourselves to be imprecise.

SOTA ZINE is not like the other girls; everything we do should be something that only we could do. We are in the business of remembering that we exist as human beings — as unique embodied subjectivities that exist in "the brief crack of light between two eternities of darkness."

— Sanjana Friedman, Editor and Publisher

## The three pillars

SOTA has three pillars, each doing something different:

**The Substack** — the publication. This is where the essays, fiction, and criticism live. This is the n+1 for tech. This is the serious intellectual product.

**The Map (sotazine.com)** — the scene-building infrastructure. This is how we find our people in SF and how they find each other. It's our City Lights Bookstore — not the writing itself, but the gathering mechanism that creates the conditions for a scene to emerge.

**The Zine** — the physical artifact. The object that bridges both and that people pass around, stamp, and keep.

The key thing: the map is not a side project or an afterthought. It's the proof of the thesis from the editor's note — that San Francisco rewards the attentive observer. We're crowdsourcing attentive observation. But it has to reflect the same editorial sensibility as the zine. If the pins are boring, it undermines everything. If they're good, it's the most compelling possible advertisement for the publication.

## On design

The red dot is your presence on the page. It moves the way you move through streets — always somewhere specific, always in motion.

We layer texture on texture, grain on grain. Nothing should look sterile. Nothing should look finished. A city is never finished, and neither is a publication about one.

Typography is architecture. Vertical titles, justified columns, monospaced coordinates — every typographic choice is a spatial choice, the same way every building on a block changes the meaning of the block.

## The name

State of the Art means two things. It means the current best — the most advanced, the most refined. And it means the condition of art itself — its state, its health, its direction. We mean both.

## Who this is for

Anyone who has walked through a city and wondered why this building is here and that one is gone. Anyone who has stood on a hill and looked at a skyline and felt something they couldn't quite name. Anyone who believes that how we build is, in the end, who we are.

Seven writers. Transit, architecture, poetry, speculative fiction, and the counter-entropic politics of building something that lasts. Edition one asks: what does it mean to be absolutely modern?

San Francisco, California. 2026.
